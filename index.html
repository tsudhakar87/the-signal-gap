Your Wearable is Reading Your Body: Is It Reading It Right?

Wearables promise precision for everyone. But for women, that promise has a hidden asterisk.

Thillai Sudhakar | June 18th, 2026

Keep scrolling

Pictured: Apple Watch, Oura Ring, Whoop Tracker, Garmin Smartwatch

Defining the Gap

These devices are missing something crucial.

If you're a woman who wears wearable devices like an Apple Watch, Oura Ring, or a Garmin smartwatch, you may have noticed some irregularities when you reference health metrics with your current menstrual cycle. For example, the app might mistakenly attribute your high heart rate as a result of general stress, rather than you being in the follicular phase of your cycle.

While wearable companies implicitly market their products as accurate and reliable for all users1, these inconsistencies in how your device interprets measurements across different hormonal states – like your menstrual or menopausal phase, pregnancy, contraceptive usage – are more common than you may think. Heart rate variability (HRV) specifically, for example, varies quite a lot. Studies have found that the HRV measurements collected by wearables differ across the menstrual cycle, generally being higher at the beginning and lower near the end2. HRV also tends to decline with menopause and contraceptive usage2–but does your watch really know about that when it pings you about a higher than normal heart rate?

What you’re experiencing is part of a larger pattern, representing a long-standing gap in women’s health research: the datasets that these devices are trained on are skewed towards male demographics. Women and minority groups weren’t even required to be included in clinical trials until the NIH Revitalization Act of 19933. While this was a major stride in improving equitable health research, our current legal frameworks still don’t fully account for all the variables that matter–to be specific, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration’s (FDA) requirements don’t include clinical validation against hormonal status, let alone sex- or gender-specific differences, before these devices are cleared to go to market. This has led to gaps in not only our understanding of various hormonal cycles, mechanisms, and diseases, but also in how the FDA regulates and approves devices with respect to sex and hormonal factors.

"Current regulatory policies are not keeping pace with the gaps caused by historical underrepresentation of women and gender minorities in clinical research."

If we want to progress in eliminating institutional sexism, though, we’ll need to examine current regulatory processes more closely first.

"Wellness" vs. "Medical" Devices

The FDA regulates devices, but not their blind spots.

While the FDA doesn’t regulate basic “wellness” devices, it does regulate medical devices–those that explicitly diagnose and mitigate medical conditions, such as continuous glucose monitors, electrocardiogram (ECG) monitoring systems, blood pressure monitoring systems, and body temperature software4. But here’s where it can get confusing: the line between these two types of devices often runs right through the same device. Take the Apple Watch, for example: its sleep tracking and activity logging fall under general wellness, but its ECG and atrial fibrillation detection features are considered medical device features5.

ECG is the primary method for HRV assessment because it directly measures the electrical activity of the heart. In 2020, Apple submitted their statistical analysis plan (SAP) to the FDA for an updated clearance of their ECG feature6. While Apple did include sex as a variable they planned to validate against in their clinical trials, they didn’t include hormonal status anywhere– calling into question just how consistent the HRV readings are for a user who is on their period versus a user who is in menopause, which Apple isn’t exactly required to have the answer for.

Clearance Pathways

Manufacturers are not required to validate against hormonal variability.

This gap doesn’t just apply to the Apple Watch, though, because the root of the problem is institutional. Consider the following pathways wearable devices can go through for clearance. Most wearable features go through 510(k) clearance, where manufacturers just have to show their device is ‘substantially equivalent’ to something already approved, and they’re not always required to undergo clinical validation7. A smaller number of higher-risk devices go through Premarket Approval (PMA), which requires actual clinical data but is reserved for devices where a malfunction could have serious consequences7. Neither of these pathways currently requires testing across hormonal states–a device just needs to perform well on average across whoever was in the study.

There are two ways the FDA oversees the requirements in these pathways: guidances, which act as outlined recommendations, and regulations, which are legally enforceable via the Code of Federal Regulations (CFR). In a guidance published last year8, the FDA stated that its objective was to “encourage the consideration of sex and associated covariates… during the study design stage”, but this is not codified in the CFR, so manufacturers aren’t required to take this into account. This means that your smartwatch may not perform as it’s expected to for everyone else when you’re, say, pregnant or on the pill or menopausal, because it was never actually tested to do so.

Significance

The Medical Stakes

Inaccurate readings in wearable devices could lead to real health consequences.

Thermoregulation

One such consequence is missed fevers, as one study reported that menstrual cycle phase may be contextually crucial to identify abnormal temperature readings9.

Insulin Fluctuations

Glucose monitors are another example: a patient’s insulin needs shift throughout the menstrual cycle, but current dosing algorithms are not calibrated for this fact and have reportedly unnecessarily increased insulin dosage levels10.

The takeaways are clear–the data you’re trusting to make health decisions may be less accurate for you than for someone whose hormones don’t fluctuate the same way. With so many pre-existing gaps in women’s health research and regulation that are only widening, it’s important to take action towards healthcare equity. But you may be asking: where to start?

Who's working on it

Organizations already pushing for change

Several organizations are already working at the intersection of women’s health and federal policy, though not many engage with the FDA directly. The Society for Women’s Health Research (SWHR), however, regularly submits comments on FDA guidance and pushes for sex-specific data requirements in medical research11.

Society for Women's Health Research (SWHR)

The Society for Women's Health Research (SWHR) is a national nonprofit founded in 1990 with the mission of advancing women's health through research on biological sex differences. Over the past three decades, it has become one of the primary organizations pushing federal agencies, including the FDA and NIH, to require that sex be treated as a variable in research design, data analysis, and reporting. Their policy work includes submitting formal comments on FDA guidance documents, advocating for sex-specific language in legislation, and participating in coalitions on issues ranging from clinical trial enrollment to maternal health.

Action Items

What needs to change--and how you can push for it.

What we should urge the FDA to do is twofold. First, and ideally, testing across hormonal states should become a baseline expectation for any wearable feature that outputs health data people rely on, especially higher-risk features like glucose monitoring or heart rhythm detection. This should be enforced not only through guidances, but through the CFR as well.

Second, and more achievable, manufacturers should have to tell us whether they tested for hormonal variability at all when seeking clearance approval. Currently, consumers really have no way of knowing if a device’s accuracy claims were ever checked against different hormonal factors, and that – even if they didn’t test it as such – is crucial to our ability to make informed choices about the wearables we buy and wear.
